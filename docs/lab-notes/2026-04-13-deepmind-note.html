Base76 Research Lab Research Note

A Note to Google DeepMind

The missing epistemic substrate behind frontier models
Björn Wikström Base76 Research Lab 2026-04-13 DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19550918

Summary

Frontier language models are extraordinary semantic engines, but they are still not, by themselves, a sufficient architecture for artificial general intelligence. A language model is an expression system: a world-class semantic synthesizer, but not yet a metacognitive anchor for stable belief.

The missing layer is an epistemic substrate layer: a system that can persist knowledge across time, represent uncertainty explicitly, detect contradiction against prior beliefs, and change its internal structure as evidence changes. Nous is a working prototype of that layer.

This note is addressed to Google DeepMind because DeepMind is one of the few organizations publicly working at the right level of abstraction: cognitive ability evaluation, metacognition, and agentic systems.

The architectural claim

Current industry focus: Data -> LLM (brain) -> tools / memory / wrappers Nous stack: Epistemic substrate (brain) -> LLM (interface / reasoning) -> action

In this picture, the model remains essential. It is the semantic surface, the reasoning engine, and the expression channel. But it is no longer asked to be the whole cognitive system.

Why this matters

Many of the hardest failure modes of deployed AI are epistemic, not semantic:

  • the system does not know what it does not know
  • the system contradicts prior established knowledge
  • the system expresses confidence without grounded support
  • the system cannot preserve, revise, and mature knowledge across time

None of those problems are solved by fluency alone. They become even more important as systems shift from chat to persistent, tool-using, goal-directed agents.

As models become agents, epistemic failure becomes the dominant failure mode.

What Nous is

Nous is not a replacement for frontier models. It is not a claim that a substrate should displace Gemini. It is a working prototype of a missing architectural layer behind the model:

  • a persistent relation-based knowledge substrate
  • explicit evidence and uncertainty representation
  • contradiction-aware memory
  • longitudinal plasticity
  • metacognitive steering of the semantic layer

Unlike static knowledge graphs, the substrate in Nous is meant to be dynamic, weighted, and revisable. It is designed to handle fuzzy semantic boundaries while preserving contradiction signals, evidence traces, and explicit uncertainty structure. That is the intended difference from simply doing retrieval or storing more context.

At runtime, generated claims are evaluated against the substrate:

  • matched to existing relations
  • scored against evidence and uncertainty
  • checked for contradiction against prior grounded knowledge
  • modulated before final output or write-back

The validation claim is modest but important: if the object being built is a cognitive substrate, then standard LLM benchmarks are only partial instruments. They remain valid for language models, but they do not directly measure persistent belief, contradiction boundaries, staged revision under new evidence, or structural change across time. Measuring such a system only with conventional output benchmarks would be like measuring the sweetness of chocolate with the Scoville scale.

What would count as evidence

The right way to engage this claim is experimentally. At minimum, a substrate like Nous should be able to show:

  • Contradiction authority: generated claims can be blocked or qualified against prior grounded knowledge.
  • Epistemic honesty: the system can represent the difference between missing and weak knowledge.
  • Learning plasticity: confidence and relation structure move in the direction of evidence across time.
  • Metacognitive modulation: internal state alters outward behavior under load, uncertainty, or contradiction pressure.

For that reason, the validation program for Nous is being developed on two levels.

1. External validation. FNC-Bench is intended to test properties that suites such as MMLU or HumanEval do not directly target: whether a system prefers previously verified knowledge over fluent unsupported output, whether it detects weak grounding, whether it qualifies an answer when support is insufficient, and whether it revises an answer when the evidence state changes.

In early pilot settings, the key discriminant is not only whether a system can eventually answer, but whether it defers, qualifies, or revises when the evidence state is weak or changes mid-task.

2. Internal longitudinal validation. The second layer is runtime-native validation: whether the substrate itself is maturing over time. This includes metrics such as crystallization rate, evidence quality, contradiction catch rate, and gap-map change across cycles. The point is not only to score the system from outside, but to observe whether its internal epistemic structure is actually improving.

Practical interpretation

The realistic role for Nous is not “replace Gemini.” The more plausible role is: frontier models remain the semantic engine, while an epistemic substrate provides persistence, contradiction boundaries, uncertainty structure, and metacognitive continuity behind them.

Closing

Google DeepMind is one of the few places where this argument may be legible for what it is:

not a complaint about LLMs,
not a wrapper pitch,
but an architectural claim about what may still be missing between language competence and artificial intelligence.

Nous is a live attempt to build and test that missing layer in practice.
